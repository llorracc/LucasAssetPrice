It may surprise you that the equilibrium price of trees today does not depend on the expected level of fruit output in the future. If the weather was bad this year, but is expected to return to normal next year (and, by deÔ¨Ånition, is expected to be equal to normal in subsequent years), you might think that the price today would mostly reÔ¨Çect the ‚Äònormal‚Äô value of fruit prodution that the trees produce, not the (temporarily low) value that happens to obtain today.

The above derivation says that intuition is wrong: Today‚Äôs price depends only on today‚Äôs output.

Nevertheless, the logic (higher future output is a reason for higher current prices) is not wrong; but it is (exactly) counterbalanced by another, and subtler, fact: Since future consumption will equal future fruit output, higher expected fruit output means lower marginal utility of consumption in that future period of (more) abundant fruit (basically, people are starving today, which reduces the attractiveness of cutting their consumption to buy trees that will produce more in a period when they expect not to be starving). These two forces are the manifestation of the (pure) income eÔ¨Äect and substitution eÔ¨Äect in this model (there is no human wealth, and therefore no human wealth eÔ¨Äect). In our assumed special case of logarithmic utility, income and substitution eÔ¨Äects are of the same size and opposite sign so the two forces exactly oÔ¨Äset.

7 Aggregate Returns Versus Individual Returns

One of the subtler entries in Aristotle¬†[350 BC]‚Äôs catalog of common human reasoning errors was the ‚Äòfallacy of composition,‚Äô in which the reasoner supposes that if a proposition is true of each element of a whole, then it must be true of the whole.

The Lucas model provides a counterexample. From the standpoint of any individual (atomistic) agent, it is quite true that a decision to save one more unit will yield greater future resources, in the amount . But from the standpoint of the society as a whole, if everyone decided to do the same thing (save one more unit), there would be no eÔ¨Äect on aggregate resources in period . Put another way, for any individual agent, it appears that the ‚Äòmarginal product of capital‚Äô is , but for the society as a whole the marginal product of capital is zero.

The proposition that the return for society as a whole must be the same as the return that is available to individuals is an error because it implicitly assumes that there are no general equilibrium eÔ¨Äects of a generalized desire to save more (or, more broadly, there is no interaction between the decisions one person makes and the decisions of another person). The Lucas model provides a counterexample in which, if everyone‚Äôs preferences change (e.g., goes down for everyone), the price of the future asset is aÔ¨Äected ‚Äì indeed, it is aÔ¨Äected in a way that is suÔ¨Écient to exactly counteract the increased desire for ownership of future dividends (since there is a Ô¨Åxed supply of assets to be owned, the demand must be reconciled with that preexisting supply).
